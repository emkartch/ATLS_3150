Universal Design: Definition and Goals

A Universal Design approach considers a diverse range of user abilities in order to create a design that meets the needs of as many people as possible, including those with disabilities.

Universal Design advocates for people to consider this range of user abilities from the start of the design process instead of at the end, like what often happens with accessibility, and to maintain this focus throughout the entire design process.

Note about Universal Design

Universal design is a design approach, not a checklist.

Accessibility vs. Universal Design

Accessible Design and Universal Design are different for many reasons. For example, Universal Design is considered from the start while Accessible Design is usually something separate that follows/meets certain standards or is added after the fact.

Then what is accessibility? Its making sure something is easy to obtain or use for those with disabilities.

What is a disability?

Ability + Barrier = Disability

There are a wide range of disabilities. For example:

Visual Impairments

Range from complete blindness, to low vision, to color blindness.

Mobile or Dexterity Impairments

Includes mobility challenges such as paraplegia/quadriplegia, cerebral palsy, or multiple sclerosis. They might also have difficulty with fine motor control due to arthritis, Parkinson's disease, or simply old age.

Auditory Impairments

Ranges from partial to total hearing loss.

Cognitive Impairments

This is the least understood issue in web accessibility, ranging from severe learning disabilities to low literacy or numeracy skills, dyslexia, and cultural and language differences. There is an incredible range of ability when it comes to cognitive impairments.

Accessibility vs. Universal Design: Who is it for?

Accessibility: For people with disabilities.

Universal Design: For people with a range of abilities.

Norman's Design Concepts

Affordance

Norman did not come up with the term “affordance,” but he is credited with the concept of “perceived affordance.” “Perceived affordance” is how users interpret and perceive the function of a device. If someone has to think about how to operate a device, that usually means the “perceived affordance” of the device is not clear enough.

In order to provide access to a wide variety of users, it is important that users be able to easily understand the function of your device.

Constraints

Constraints limit the actions of users in order to prevent error, misuse, damage to the device, or harm to the user or others.

Norman divides constraints into two categories, physical and psychological; Norman also breaks up the psychological category into three categories: semantic, cultural, and logical.

Physical
Cars are designed with physical properties to prevent users from changing gears in an automatic car without pushing on the brakes.
Psychological: Semantic
Semantic constraints warn of improper use through language, symbols, or auditory cues. For example, cars are designed to chime repeatedly if it detects enough weight in the passenger seat, but no bulked seatbelt.
Psychological: Cultural
Cultural constraints refer to conventions learned through experience. For example, we know from convention that we pay for purchases at a cashier, typically at the front of the store.
Psychological: Logical
Logical constraints refer to times when users may lack semantic or cultural constraints, so users instead deduce the answer through logic. For example, a steering wheel being placed in front of the driver's seat because it makes logical sense for the driver to have access to the steering controls.

Feedback

Users must receive feedback after every action performed in order to let them know if their action was successful or not. For example, a cursor changing into a spinner while the computer is loading.

Visibility

Visibility refers to the idea that effective design involves making applicable items visible to the user and hiding non-relevant operations. For example, if a user is trying to load a file into a program that only takes PDFs, the computer will gray out all non-PDF files.

Mapping

Mapping is the idea that the controls for a device will closely resemble their effect. For example, a stovetop where the dials are arranged in the same order as the burners to let the user know which dial operates which burner.

7 Principles of Universal Design

There are 7 core principles of Universal Design:

  1. Equitable Use
    • Design provides the same means of use for anyone accessing resources: identical whenever possible, equivalent when not.
  2. Flexibility in Use
    • Design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities. For example, left or right handed access and use.
  3. Simple and Intuitive Use
    • Design is easy to understand, regardless of the user's experience, knowledge, language skills, or current concentration level.
  4. Perceptible Information
    • Design communicates necessary information effectively to the user, regardless of ambient conditions or the user's sensory abilities.
  5. Tolerance for Error
    • Design minimizes hazards and the adverse consequences of accidental or unintended actions.
  6. Low Physical Effort
    • Design can be used efficiently and comfortably. For example, online classes allow consumption of information with little eye strain and fatigue.
  7. Size and Space for Approach and Use
    • Design provides for approach, reach, manipulation, and use regardless of user's body size, posture, or mobility. For example, online learning platforms allow for the use/integration of assistive devices such as a JAWS reader.

Website Accessibility

Web Standards and Coding Basics

Web Standards means creating web pages with the syntax and code elements that will be recognized by the range of browsers and platforms that are standards-compliant.

Additional Information about Web Standards

Web Standards also include the separation of content from presentation (Using HTML for structure and content, and CSS for styling).

Worldwide Web Consortium (W3C)

The W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) develops web accessibility standards and guidelines for web and software developers.

WCAG

Organized under 4 foundational principles with the acronym POUR.

Perceivable
Information and user interface components must be presented to users in ways they can see or hear.
Operable
User interface components and navigation must be designed so that users of all abilities can interact with them, meaning they must be able to support assistive technologies such as screen readers.
Understandable
The information and the operation of the user interface must communicate clearly and consistently to make the content readable.
Robust
Content must be written so that it can be easily interpreted by a wide variety of users and user agents, including assistive technologies.
WAI-ARIA

ARIA attributes bridge the gap to address accessibility issues that cannot be managed with native HTML.

ARIA was created for screen reader users, and users who cannot use a mouse and must rely on their keyboard.

What can ARIA do?
Roles

Roles are used to define a type of user interface (UI) element. Once a role is set for an element, it does not change.

States and Properties

States and properties can be applied to elements and are used together to support existing ARIA roles on a page. States can change on their own or with user interaction while properties rarely change once they're set.

The five rules of ARIA
  1. Use native HTML at all times.
  2. Do not change native HTML semantics unless you absolutely have to.
  3. All interactive ARIA roles must be keyboard accessible.
  4. For all elements that are focusable, do not ever add role=“presentation” or aria-hidden=“true”.
  5. You must give all interactive elements an accessible name.

Correct use of HTML Hierarchy

HTML utilizes a series of heading options when coding ranging from h1 (heading 1) to h6 (heading 6) and it is important to use them hierarchically so that the content is accurately communicated to assistive technology and any code-reading systems that analyze web pages; for example, search engine web crawlers.

In order to accomplish this, you may need to including headings that may be redundant for visual users, but you can use CSS to hide those headings in a way that doesn't break the hierarchy, doesn't add more clutter to the page, and makes sure the heading can still be recognized by assistive technology and code-reading systems.

In addition to headings, HTML lists convey a hierarchical structure.

  • Unordered lists are for content having no order of sequence or importance.
  • Ordered lists suggest sequence, order, or ranking. List items are typically prepended with a number, letter, Roman numeral, etc.
  • Description lists are for key:value pairs, such as terms and definitions in a glossary or questions and answers in a FAQ.

Use of Relative Units

Instead of using pixels, use em (a unit of measurement that indicates size relative to the size of the font) which will allow your website to correctly adapt to a variety of screen sizes.

Elements to Consider in Accessible Web Design

Keyboard Navigation

For users who can only use keyboard navigation, a website needs to be accessible for those who can't, for one reason or another, use a mouse.

Color Contrast

For users with low vision or color blindness, checking color contrast throughout the color blindness spectrum.

Fonts

When choosing a font, make sure it is legible, and size it to be easily readable.

Alt Text

Add alt text to all images that describes the image, but doesn't give additional information that those not using assistive technology wouldn't get.

If an image needs more than 100 characters to be accurately described, move the description into its own paragraph where both those using and not using assistive technology can access it.
